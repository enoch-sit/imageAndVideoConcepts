Concept 1

The Score Function

At every point in space, the score tells you which direction to move to reach higher probability — where the real data lives.

What is it?

Given a probability density function p(x) that describes how likely each point x is to be "real data," the score function is defined as:

s(x) = ∇ₓ log p(x)

This is the gradient (direction of steepest ascent) of the log probability density. It's a vector field: at every point x, it gives a direction and magnitude pointing toward regions of higher data likelihood.

Why the log?

Taking the logarithm transforms multiplication into addition and turns tiny probability values into manageable numbers. Critically, ∇ log p(x) = ∇p(x) / p(x), so the score is a normalised gradient — it doesn't depend on the overall scale of p(x), which means we never need to know the normalising constant Z.

Concept 2

Learning the Score by Denoising

Diffusion models sidestep the intractable normalising constant by learning the score directly through a denoising objective.

CNoise prediction ≈ score

The Normalising Constant Problem

To compute p(x) exactly, we'd need:

p(x) = p̃(x) / Z   where   Z = ∫ p̃(x) dx

This integral Z is intractable for complex data like images (imagine integrating over all possible pixel combinations). But the score ∇ log p(x) is independent of Z, because log Z is just a constant whose gradient is zero!

Denoising Score Matching

The key insight: if we train a neural network to predict the noise ε that was added to a clean sample, the network's output is proportional to the score at the noisy point:

s_θ(x̃, σ) ≈ ∇ log p_σ(x̃) = −ε / σ²

So by simply learning to denoise, the network implicitly learns the score function at every noise level — no normalising constant needed!

Concept 3

The Neural Compass

The trained network acts as a compass in noise-space, guiding iterative steps from pure noise to a coherent image.

From Noise to Image, Step by Step

At generation time, we start with pure Gaussian noise x_T and iteratively refine it. At each step t, the network predicts the score at the current noise level σ_t, and we take a small step in that direction:

x_{t-1} = x_t + η · s_θ(x_t, σ_t) + √(2η) · z

This is Langevin dynamics: follow the score (climb toward data) plus a small random perturbation z for exploration. As σ decreases through the schedule, the steps become more precise and the image sharpens.

Why It Works

The magic lies in the multi-scale nature. At high noise, the score captures global structure (overall shapes, layout). At low noise, it captures fine details (textures, edges). The neural network is trained at all noise levels simultaneously, so it learns a complete map from any amount of noise back to clean data.
