01

The Two-Copy Architecture

ControlNet adds a trainable copy of the SD U-Net encoder that accepts the spatial condition image. The frozen backbone is preserved; the copies are added via zero-initialised convolutions.

Interactive: ControlNet Architecture
Click the segments to understand what is frozen vs trainable. The spatial condition flows through the trainable copy and is added to the frozen decoder.

Why Copy only the Encoder?

The ControlNet copy mirrors only the encoder and bottleneck of the U-Net, not the decoder. This is because spatial structure (pose, edges) is encoded in the early/bottleneck representations, while the decoder focuses on reconstruction quality. Copying only half of the U-Net keeps the added parameter count below 600M.

Parameter split Frozen SD U-Net backbone: 860M params (locked)
Trainable ControlNet copy: ≈580M params (encoder + bottleneck)
Zero-conv layers: near-zero params at init
Why freeze the backbone?
Training the backbone from scratch or fine-tuning it risks catastrophic forgetting — the model would lose the rich image priors learned from LAION. Freezing preserves the quality of SD 1.5 completely; only the spatial conditioning logic is learned from scratch.

03

Zero-Conv: Stable Initialisation

The key innovation that makes ControlNet training stable from the start is "zero-convolution" — 1×1 convolutional layers initialised with zero weights and zero biases.

What is a Zero-Conv Layer?

A 1×1 convolution with weights W and bias b initialised to exactly zero. At training iteration zero, the layer outputs zero regardless of input. This means the ControlNet copy contributes nothing to the frozen U-Net at the start — the baseline generation quality is preserved during the first gradient update.

Zero-conv at initialisation (t=0) output = W · input + b where W=0, b=0 → output=0
After first gradient step: W and b begin to grow away from 0

Why Does This Matter?

Without zero-conv, at initialisation the ControlNet copy outputs random noise that is immediately added to the frozen U-Net's skip connections — destabilising early training. With zero-conv, the ControlNet is silently passthrough at step 0; gradients only push it to contribute once they establish a productive direction.

Alternative: LoRA adapters
The same stability principle is used by LoRA fine-tuning: a low-rank decomposition A·B where B is initialised to zero, ensuring zero-delta at initialisation. ControlNet and LoRA are both additive fine-tuning methods — they add on top of a frozen base.
