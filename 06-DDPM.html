01

The Forward Process

Starting from a real image x₀, the forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise over T = 1000 steps until the image is pure noise x_T.

Interactive: Forward Diffusion Noising Ladder
Drag the step slider to add noise to the image. At step 1000 the signal is indistinguishable from Gaussian noise.
0

The Forward Kernel

Each step adds a small amount of Gaussian noise proportional to a variance parameter β_t. The key mathematical convenience is that we can skip straight to any step t using the "reparameterised" closed-form equation.

Single-step forward kernel q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t; √(1−β_t)·x_{t-1}, β_t·I)
Jump directly to step t (ᾱ_t = ∏from s=1 to t of (1−β_s)) q(x_t | x₀) = N(x_t; √ᾱ_t·x₀, (1−ᾱ_t)·I)
x_t = √ᾱ_t · x₀ + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε, ε ∼ N(0, I)

Signal-to-Noise Ratio

As t → T, the signal coefficient √ᾱ_t → 0 and the noise coefficient √(1−ᾱ_t) → 1. At t = T ≈ 1000, the original image is essentially gone and only isotropic Gaussian noise remains.

Why this is useful for training
For any input image x₀ and any time step t, we can compute the exact noisy version x_t in one step. This means we can train the U-Net to denoise by sampling (x₀, t) pairs, adding noise with the closed form, and asking the network to predict the noise that was added.
02

The Reverse Process & U-Net

The reverse process learns to remove noise one step at a time. A U-Net network is trained to predict the noise ε added at each step, conditioned on the current noisy image and the time step t.

Noise Prediction Objective

The network ε_θ is trained to predict the noise ε given the noisy sample x_t and the step index t. This is called the "simple objective" by Ho et al. — it outperforms the full ELBO in practice.

DDPM Training Objective L = E_{x₀, ε, t} [ ‖ε − ε_θ(x_t, t)‖² ]

During sampling, we run the learned reverse step repeatedly:
x_{t-1} = (1/√α_t) · (x_t − β_t/√(1−ᾱ_t) · ε_θ(x_t, t)) + σ_t · z

U-Net Architecture

The denoiser uses a U-Net: convolutional encoder (downsamples), bottleneck with self-attention, convolutional decoder (upsamples), and skip connections that pass spatial details directly from encoder to decoder.

U-Net Signal Flow
Input (x_t, t) → [Encoder Block ×4] ──────────────┐
                       ↓ (downscale)            skip connections
                  [Bottleneck + Attention]           │
                       ↓ (upscale)                   │
                 [Decoder Block ×4] ←────────────────┘
                       ↓
                Predicted noise ε̂

Key limitation → solved by DDIM
DDPM requires all T = 1000 reverse steps to generate one image — one U-Net forward pass per step. At ~50ms per step on a GPU, that's ~50 seconds per image. DDIM (ICLR 2021) reformulates the sampler non-Markovianly, reducing to 20–50 steps without retraining the U-Net.
03

The Noise Schedule

The sequence β₁, β₂, …, β_T controls how quickly signal is destroyed. The shape of this schedule critically affects generation quality.

Interactive: Compare Noise Schedules
Toggle between Linear (DDPM) and Cosine (improved DDPM) schedules. The cosine schedule preserves signal longer in middle timesteps where the image content matters most.

Linear Schedule (Ho et al., 2020)

β_t increases linearly from β_1 = 0.0001 to β_T = 0.02 over T = 1000 steps. This works well for 256×256 images but destroys signal too fast at higher resolutions — at high resolution, the image becomes unrecognisable too early in the diffusion chain.

Linear Schedule β_t = 0.0001 + (t−1) × (0.02−0.0001) / (T−1)

Cosine Schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021)

The cosine schedule is defined via the cumulative product ᾱ_t directly, ensuring the signal is preserved more gently in middle steps where structural information is richest. This schedule is now standard in SD 1.5, SDXL, and most production models.

Cosine Schedule ᾱ_t = cos²( (t/T + s) / (1+s) · π/2 ) (s = 0.008)
