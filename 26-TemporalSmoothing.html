for Blendshapes

Raw phoneme-driven blendshape sequences have abrupt step changes at every phoneme boundary, producing an unnatural "puppet" jitter. Temporal smoothing filters the signal to remove high-frequency artefacts while preserving the intended motion amplitude.

The Problem: Jitter from Step Changes

Phoneme-aligned blendshape weights change instantaneously at each phoneme boundary. Even with half-cosine coarticulation blending at ±30 ms, the resulting signal still has abrupt transitions. Compare the raw and smoothed signals below.

Why It Matters

At 25 fps a phoneme lasts 3–8 frames. Without smoothing, every frame transition is a potential discontinuity visible as a sudden jaw snap or lip flutter. The human visual system is acutely sensitive to facial jitter, far more so than to equivalent motion in limbs.

Gaussian & Savitzky-Golay Filters

The simplest approach: convolve the blendshape time-series with a symmetric kernel. A Gaussian kernel produces smooth but slightly blurred peaks. Savitzky-Golay fits a local polynomial — it preserves peak height better while still removing high-frequency noise.

One-Euro Adaptive Filter

Gaussian smoothing adds latency proportional to σ. For realtime applications the One-Euro filter adapts its cut-off frequency to instantaneous signal speed: slow movements get heavy smoothing, fast movements get almost none, minimising perceived lag.

One-Euro derivation (1 blendshape channel at 25 fps) d_hat[t] = α_d · (x[t]−x_hat[t−1]) + (1−α_d) · d_hat[t−1]
cutoff[t] = min_cutoff + beta · |d_hat[t]|
α(fc) = 2πfc / (2πfc + fps) (low-pass coefficient)
x_hat[t] = α(cutoff[t]) · x[t] + (1−α(cutoff[t])) · x_hat[t−1]
Recommended defaults for 25 fps lip sync
One-Euro: min_cutoff=1.5 Hz, beta=0.4. Gaussian: σ=2–3 frames. In practice, Gaussian is applied offline (post-processing on the full sequence) while One-Euro is used for real-time inference where causal filtering is required.
